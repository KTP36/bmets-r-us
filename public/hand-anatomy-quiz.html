Hand Anatomy Study Guide

The hand contains 27 bones and is one of the most important anatomy regions for healthcare students to understand. This guide reviews the carpals, metacarpals, phalanges, joints, memory tricks, and common clinical connections.

Quick fact: Each hand has 8 carpals, 5 metacarpals, and 14 phalanges.

Hand Bones Overview

RegionNumberWhat to Remember
Carpals8Small wrist bones arranged in proximal and distal rows.
Metacarpals5Palm bones numbered 1–5 from thumb to little finger.
Phalanges14Finger bones. Each finger has 3 except the thumb, which has 2.

Clinical Connections

  • Scaphoid fracture: Common wrist fracture after falling on an outstretched hand.
  • Carpal tunnel: Involves compression of the median nerve at the wrist.
  • Boxer's fracture: Often affects the 5th metacarpal neck.
  • Trigger finger: Tendon movement problem that can cause catching or locking.
  • Dupuytren contracture: Thickening of palmar fascia causing finger flexion.

Hand Muscles to Know

  • Thenar muscles move the thumb.
  • Hypothenar muscles move the little finger.
  • Lumbricals flex MCP joints.
  • Interossei abduct/adduct the fingers.

Tendons, Nerves & Blood Supply

The hand's precise movement depends on flexor and extensor tendons, three major nerves, and a rich arterial supply.

StructureFunction
Median nerveThumb, index, middle finger sensation and thenar muscles.
Ulnar nerveMost intrinsic hand muscles and little finger sensation.
Radial nerveExtension and dorsal hand sensation.
Flexor tendonsFinger flexion.
Extensor tendonsFinger extension.
Superficial & Deep Palmar ArchesMain arterial blood supply.

Common Hand Injuries & Imaging

  • Scaphoid fractures may not appear on initial X-rays.
  • Boxer's fractures involve the 5th metacarpal.
  • Mallet finger affects the extensor tendon.
  • Jersey finger affects the flexor tendon.
  • MRI is useful for soft tissue injuries; X-ray is best for fractures.

Hand Anatomy Clinical Case Studies

Use these short scenarios to connect anatomy terms to real-world healthcare thinking.

  • Case 1: A patient falls on an outstretched hand and has pain near the anatomical snuffbox. Think scaphoid fracture.
  • Case 2: Numbness and tingling affect the thumb, index, and middle fingers. Think median nerve and carpal tunnel.
  • Case 3: A patient cannot extend the tip of the finger after injury. Think mallet finger and extensor tendon injury.
  • Case 4: Pain and deformity occur near the 5th metacarpal after punching an object. Think boxer's fracture.
