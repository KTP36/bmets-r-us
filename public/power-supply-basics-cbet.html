Power Supply Basics for CBET

Power supplies are one of the most important components in biomedical equipment. Almost every device depends on converting incoming electrical power into a stable form that circuits can use.

Understanding how power supplies work helps you answer CBET questions and troubleshoot real equipment issues more effectively.

If you need a foundation first, review voltage, current, and resistance before diving deeper.

What Does a Power Supply Do?

A power supply takes incoming electrical energy and converts it into a usable and stable form for electronic circuits.

Most medical equipment receives AC power from the wall but requires DC power internally. The power supply is responsible for making that conversion.

Basic Power Supply Process

A typical power supply follows a sequence of steps:

  • AC input from the wall
  • Rectification (AC to DC conversion)
  • Filtering (smoothing the signal)
  • Voltage regulation
Step Function
AC Input Power enters the device from an outlet
Rectifier Converts AC into DC
Filter Smooths out voltage fluctuations
Regulator Maintains a stable output voltage

Rectification (AC to DC Conversion)

Rectifiers use diodes to convert alternating current into direct current.

This is a critical step because most circuits require DC power to function correctly.

Learn more about this process here: What Does a Rectifier Do?

Filtering and Smoothing

After rectification, the DC signal is not perfectly smooth. It contains ripple, which can affect circuit performance.

Capacitors are used to smooth out these fluctuations and create a more stable signal.

Learn more: What Does a Capacitor Do?

Voltage Regulation

Voltage regulators ensure that the output voltage remains constant, even if input voltage or load conditions change.

Stable voltage is critical for sensitive medical equipment.

Why This Matters in Real Equipment

If a power supply fails, a device may:

  • Not power on
  • Operate inconsistently
  • Produce inaccurate readings

Understanding each stage helps identify where the failure may be occurring.
