Start With the Big Idea

Electronics is the study of how electricity moves through a circuit and how components control that movement. Once you understand a few basic ideas, the rest starts to make more sense.

Simple way to think about it:
Voltage pushes electricity, current is the flow, and resistance slows or limits the flow.

In medical equipment, these ideas show up everywhere. Power supplies convert electricity into usable voltage. Circuit boards control signals. Sensors measure changes. Fuses protect equipment. Multimeters help technicians test what is happening inside a device.

The Three Core Concepts

Voltage

Voltage is electrical pressure. It is the push that moves electricity through a circuit.

Voltage is measured in volts, shown as V.

Current

Current is the flow of electric charge. If voltage is the push, current is what moves.

Current is measured in amps, shown as A.

Resistance

Resistance opposes current flow. More resistance means less current can flow.

Resistance is measured in ohms, shown as Ω.

Power

Power is how much electrical energy is being used or delivered by a circuit.

Power is measured in watts, shown as W.

Ohm’s Law: The Formula That Connects Everything

Ohm’s Law explains the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance.

V = I × R

That means voltage equals current multiplied by resistance.

  • V = voltage in volts
  • I = current in amps
  • R = resistance in ohms
If voltage goes up and resistance stays the same, current goes up. If resistance goes up and voltage stays the same, current goes down.
Quick example:

If a circuit has 12 volts and 6 ohms of resistance, current is 2 amps.

12 ÷ 6 = 2

How Circuits Work

A circuit is a complete path that allows electricity to flow. If the path is broken, current cannot flow.

Series Circuit

In a series circuit, components are connected one after another. Current has only one path. If one part opens or fails, the entire circuit can stop working.

Parallel Circuit

In a parallel circuit, components have separate paths. If one branch fails, another branch may still work.

Common beginner mistake:
Students often think all circuits behave the same way. Series and parallel circuits behave differently, especially when components fail.

Common Electronic Components

Components are the individual parts that make circuits work. You do not need to memorize every part at once. Start by recognizing the most common ones.

Resistor

A resistor limits or controls current flow. It protects components and helps set circuit values.

AC vs DC Current

Electricity can be AC or DC.

AC Current

AC stands for alternating current. It changes direction repeatedly. Wall outlets supply AC power.

DC Current

DC stands for direct current. It flows in one direction. Batteries and many circuit boards use DC power.

Medical equipment often receives AC power from the wall, then uses an internal power supply to convert it into DC voltages that the device can use.

Why This Matters for Biomedical Equipment

Biomedical equipment depends on electronics. Even if you are not designing circuits, you still need to understand basic electronics to troubleshoot safely and think clearly.

For example:

  • An IV pump may have power supply issues, battery issues, charging problems, or board-level faults.
  • A monitor may have display, input, power, or signal issues.
  • An ultrasound system depends on probes, signal processing, power supplies, and image display circuits.
  • An X-ray system uses higher-level electrical concepts, including power, exposure factors, and safety systems.
The goal is not to become perfect overnight. The goal is to recognize what you are looking at and understand what to check first.

Using a Multimeter

A multimeter is one of the most important tools for electronics troubleshooting.

A beginner should know how to measure:

  • Voltage to see if power is present
  • Resistance to check components or wiring
  • Continuity to see if a path is complete
Safety reminder:
Always know what you are measuring before placing meter leads on a circuit. Use the correct setting and follow proper safety procedures.

Common Beginner Mistakes

1. Trying to memorize formulas without understanding the meaning

The formula matters, but the concept matters more. Know what voltage, current, and resistance are doing.

2. Mixing up voltage and current

Voltage is the push. Current is the flow. They are related, but they are not the same thing.

3. Forgetting that resistance limits current

Higher resistance usually means less current if voltage stays the same.

4. Treating series and parallel circuits the same

Components behave differently depending on how they are connected.

5. Using a multimeter without checking the setting first

The meter setting matters. Measuring voltage, resistance, and current are different tasks.
