How the Kidneys Work

Learn kidney function in a simple, practical way. This guide explains filtration, reabsorption, urine formation, fluid balance, electrolytes, acid-base balance, and why the kidneys matter so much in nursing, TEAS, and NCLEX study.

What to notice first:

The kidneys are not just “making urine.” They are constantly deciding what the body keeps and what the body gets rid of. That decision affects fluid balance, electrolytes, blood pressure, waste removal, and acid-base balance.

What Do the Kidneys Actually Do?

The kidneys are two bean-shaped organs that constantly filter the blood. Their job is not just to make urine. They also help control fluid balance, electrolytes, blood pressure, waste removal, and acid-base balance.

In simple terms, the kidneys decide what the body should keep and what the body should get rid of. They keep useful substances like water and needed electrolytes when appropriate, and they remove waste products and excess fluid through urine.

This is why kidney function is connected to so many healthcare topics. When the kidneys are not working well, the problem may show up in urine output, lab values, swelling, blood pressure, potassium levels, or acid-base balance.

Simple way to remember it:

The kidneys filter, balance, and protect.

Why the Kidneys Matter in Nursing

Kidney function shows up everywhere in nursing because the kidneys help regulate several things that affect patient safety.

If the kidneys are not working well, you may see changes in:

  • Urine output
  • Fluid balance
  • Potassium and sodium levels
  • Blood pressure
  • Edema
  • Acid-base balance
  • BUN and creatinine
  • Medication clearance

That is why kidney function is important for NCLEX prep, TEAS science review, lab interpretation, medication safety, and real patient care.

What to notice first:

When kidney function is tested on exams, the question often focuses on what the kidneys are failing to control: fluid, electrolytes, waste, blood pressure, or acid-base balance.

The Nephron: The Functional Unit of the Kidney

The nephron is the tiny working unit inside the kidney. Each nephron helps filter blood and turn filtered fluid into urine.

You do not need to memorize every detail at first. Start by understanding the basic flow: blood is filtered, useful substances are reabsorbed, waste remains, and urine is formed.

Part Main Role What to Notice First
Glomerulus Filters blood This is where filtration begins.
Bowman’s capsule Collects filtered fluid Filtered fluid enters the nephron here.
Proximal tubule Reabsorbs a large amount of water and useful substances The body takes back what it still needs.
Loop of Henle Helps concentrate urine Important for water balance and urine concentration.
Distal tubule Fine-tunes electrolyte balance Helps adjust sodium, potassium, and other electrolytes.
Collecting duct Final concentration of urine Final adjustment before urine leaves the nephron.
What to notice first:

Filtration happens first, but reabsorption is what makes kidney function powerful. The kidneys do not simply dump everything into urine. They take back what the body still needs.

The 3 Big Steps to Understand Kidney Function

1. Filtration

Filtration happens first in the glomerulus. Blood enters the kidney, and small substances are pushed out of the bloodstream into the nephron. This filtered fluid may contain water, electrolytes, glucose, urea, and other small particles.

Big things like most blood cells and large proteins should stay in the bloodstream, not pass into the urine.

2. Reabsorption

After filtration, the kidneys take back what the body still needs. This is called reabsorption. Water, glucose, sodium, bicarbonate, and other important substances can move back into the bloodstream.

This is one of the most important kidney concepts. The kidneys do not simply remove everything. They carefully reclaim useful substances.

3. Excretion

What the body does not need stays in the nephron and leaves as urine. This includes waste products and excess fluid.

What to notice first:

If something goes wrong, ask where the process failed: filtration, reabsorption, secretion, or fluid balance.

How Urine Is Formed

  1. Blood is filtered in the glomerulus.
  2. Filtered fluid enters the nephron.
  3. Useful substances are reabsorbed back into the blood.
  4. Additional waste can be secreted into the tubule.
  5. Extra waste and excess fluid stay behind.
  6. The final product moves through the collecting ducts.
  7. Urine travels through the ureters to the bladder.
Easy memory tip:

Filtration first, then reabsorption, then waste leaves as urine.

How the Kidneys Help Control Fluid Balance

The kidneys constantly decide how much water the body should keep or remove. If the body holds on to too much fluid, swelling and elevated blood pressure can happen. If the body loses too much fluid, dehydration can happen.

This is why kidney function is tightly connected to:

  • Edema
  • Blood pressure
  • Urine output
  • Daily weights
  • Intake and output tracking
  • Fluid overload
  • Dehydration
What to notice first:

If the question mentions swelling, weight gain, decreased urine output, or high blood pressure, think about fluid balance and kidney function.

How the Kidneys Affect Electrolytes

The kidneys help regulate electrolytes like potassium and sodium. When kidney function gets worse, dangerous electrolyte abnormalities can happen.

  • Potassium: can build up and increase the risk of dangerous heart rhythm problems.
  • Sodium: can become too high or too low depending on fluid shifts and kidney function.
  • Bicarbonate: helps with acid-base balance.
  • Calcium and phosphorus: can also be affected by kidney disease.

This is why renal function matters so much in lab review and patient monitoring.

Important:

High potassium is a major kidney-related safety concern because it can affect the heart. If an exam question mentions kidney disease and potassium, pay close attention.

Kidneys and Acid-Base Balance

The kidneys help maintain acid-base balance by handling bicarbonate and hydrogen ions. This process works more slowly than the lungs, but it is very important for long-term pH control.

This is why kidney function connects closely to ABG interpretation. The lungs are linked to carbon dioxide, while the kidneys are linked to bicarbonate.

What to notice first:

In ABG questions, PaCO2 is respiratory and HCO3 is metabolic. The kidneys help control the metabolic side.

Review this connection in the ABG Normal Values Guide.

Kidneys and Blood Pressure

The kidneys help regulate blood pressure by controlling fluid volume and hormone pathways. If the body keeps too much fluid, blood pressure may rise. If the body loses too much fluid, blood pressure may fall.

Kidney problems and blood pressure problems are closely connected. Poor kidney function can contribute to high blood pressure, and long-term high blood pressure can damage the kidneys.

What to notice first:

Blood pressure and kidney function affect each other. If one is abnormal, look for clues about the other.

Review related patient assessment basics in the Normal Vital Signs Chart.

Key Kidney Terms Students Should Know

  • Nephron: functional unit of the kidney.
  • Glomerulus: site of filtration.
  • Reabsorption: taking useful substances back into the blood.
  • Secretion: moving certain substances into the tubule for removal.
  • Excretion: removal of waste from the body.
  • Urine output: a key sign of kidney function and fluid balance.
  • Creatinine: common lab used to assess kidney function.
  • BUN: another lab used in renal assessment.
  • GFR: estimated measure of kidney filtering function.

Common Clinical Connections

Kidney concepts are easier to remember when you connect them to real patient care. If the kidneys are not filtering and balancing correctly, you may see:

  • Low urine output
  • Fluid overload
  • Swelling
  • Rising creatinine
  • Elevated BUN
  • Electrolyte imbalances
  • Changes in blood pressure
  • Acid-base imbalance

These are exactly the kinds of patterns nursing students, TEAS learners, and NCLEX-style questions often test.

What to notice first:

Look for the pattern. Low urine output plus rising creatinine plus fluid overload points toward kidney function problems.

How to Think About Kidney Function on Exams

On exams like the NCLEX, TEAS, or nursing fundamentals tests, kidney questions usually test recognition rather than memorization.

Instead of asking yourself only “What is the kidney?” ask what problem the question is pointing toward.

  • Low urine output may suggest poor kidney perfusion or kidney dysfunction.
  • High potassium may suggest impaired kidney function.
  • Fluid overload may suggest the kidneys are not removing fluid effectively.
  • Rising creatinine suggests declining kidney function.
  • Abnormal bicarbonate may connect kidney function to acid-base balance.
What to notice first:

Look for what the kidneys are failing to control: fluid, electrolytes, waste, blood pressure, or acid-base balance.

Common Mistakes Students Make With Kidney Function

  • Thinking the kidneys only make urine.
  • Forgetting that the kidneys help control potassium.
  • Confusing filtration with reabsorption.
  • Ignoring urine output as an assessment clue.
  • Forgetting the kidney connection to blood pressure.
  • Forgetting the kidney connection to acid-base balance.
  • Trying to memorize nephron parts without understanding the sequence.
Important:

Kidney questions often become easier when you slow down and identify the function being tested.

How to Study Kidney Function Better

  • Learn filtration, reabsorption, secretion, and excretion in order.
  • Connect anatomy to function instead of memorizing isolated terms.
  • Review renal-related lab values like BUN, creatinine, potassium, and GFR.
  • Practice questions that mix anatomy, function, and clinical meaning.
  • Use repetition until the sequence becomes automatic.
  • Connect kidney function to vital signs, ABGs, and lab values.
Study strategy:

Do not study the kidneys as an isolated organ. Study them as part of fluid balance, lab values, vital signs, and acid-base control.

Frequently Asked Questions

What do the kidneys do?

The kidneys filter blood, remove waste, balance fluids, regulate electrolytes, help control blood pressure, support acid-base balance, and produce urine.

What is the nephron?

The nephron is the functional unit of the kidney. It filters blood and helps with reabsorption, secretion, and urine formation.

Why is urine output important?

Urine output is an important clue about kidney function, hydration, perfusion, and fluid balance.

Why does potassium matter with kidney function?

The kidneys help remove excess potassium. If kidney function worsens, potassium can rise and increase the risk of heart rhythm problems.

How are kidneys related to ABGs?

The kidneys help control bicarbonate, which is the metabolic component of acid-base balance. That is why kidney function connects to ABG interpretation.
