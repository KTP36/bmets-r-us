Normal Vital Signs Chart (Adults & Pediatrics)

Normal vital signs are one of the first things healthcare students learn because they help show whether a patient is stable or may be developing a problem. This guide reviews normal adult vital signs, pediatric ranges, abnormal findings, and what to notice first when interpreting patient assessment data.

This page is useful for nursing students, TEAS prep, medical assistants, EMT students, and early healthcare learners reviewing heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, temperature, oxygen saturation, and pain assessment.

What to notice first:

Vital signs are not just numbers to memorize. Look for patterns. Changes in heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, and temperature often give early clues that a patient is improving, compensating, or declining.

Normal Vital Signs Chart

Use this quick reference chart to review common normal ranges for adults and general pediatric patients. Pediatric vital signs vary by age, so infants and toddlers often have higher heart rates and respiratory rates than adults.

Vital Sign Normal Adult Range General Pediatric Range Why It Matters
Heart Rate 60–100 bpm 70–120 bpm Shows how fast the heart is beating and may reflect stress, pain, fever, dehydration, bleeding, shock, or cardiac issues.
Respiratory Rate 12–20 breaths/min 20–30 breaths/min Helps assess breathing, oxygenation, respiratory effort, and early signs of decline.
Blood Pressure About 90/60 to 120/80 mmHg Varies by age and size Reflects circulation and perfusion. Low or high readings can signal important changes.
Temperature 97–99°F (36.1–37.2°C) Similar to adults May indicate infection, inflammation, environmental exposure, or metabolic changes.
Oxygen Saturation (SpO2) 95–100% 95–100% Shows how well oxygen is being carried in the blood.
Pain Score 0–10 scale Often age-based scales Pain can affect heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, and overall patient status.
Easy memory reminder:

Learn adult ranges first. Then remember that younger pediatric patients usually have faster heart rates and respiratory rates.

What to Notice First With Vital Signs

The biggest mistake beginners make is memorizing ranges without understanding what the numbers mean. Normal ranges matter, but the patient pattern matters more.

  • Fast heart rate: Think pain, fever, dehydration, anxiety, bleeding, shock, or compensation.
  • Fast respiratory rate: Think respiratory distress, fever, pain, anxiety, or metabolic changes.
  • Low blood pressure: Think dehydration, bleeding, poor perfusion, sepsis, or shock.
  • Low oxygen saturation: Think impaired oxygenation, poor gas exchange, sensor placement, or poor perfusion.
  • Fever: Think infection, inflammation, or illness response.
What to notice first:

A single abnormal number is a clue. A pattern of abnormal vital signs is much more important.

Age Matters in Pediatrics

Pediatric vital signs are not interpreted the same way as adult vital signs. Children often have faster heart rates and respiratory rates, especially when they are younger. That means a value that looks high for an adult may be normal for a child.

Age Group Typical Heart Rate Typical Respiratory Rate What to Notice
Infant 100–160 bpm 30–60 breaths/min Higher rates are expected compared with adults.
Toddler 90–150 bpm 24–40 breaths/min Still faster than adult values.
Preschool Child 80–140 bpm 22–34 breaths/min Heart rate and respiratory rate begin trending downward.
School-Age Child 70–120 bpm 18–30 breaths/min Closer to adult values but still age-dependent.
Adolescent 60–100 bpm 12–20 breaths/min Often similar to adult ranges.
What to notice first:

The younger the child, the higher the normal heart rate and respiratory rate tend to be.

What Each Vital Sign Means

Heart Rate

Heart rate tells you how many times the heart beats per minute. A higher heart rate may occur with fever, pain, anxiety, dehydration, blood loss, exercise, or shock. A low heart rate may be normal in some athletes, but it can also suggest medication effects or conduction problems.

Respiratory Rate

Respiratory rate is one of the most important vital signs because it can change early when a patient is declining. Fast breathing may indicate fever, pain, respiratory distress, anxiety, or metabolic problems. Slow breathing may occur with sedation, overdose, or neurologic issues.

What to notice first:

Do not overlook respiratory rate. It is often one of the earliest signs that something is changing.

Blood Pressure

Blood pressure measures the force of blood pushing against artery walls. Low blood pressure may reduce perfusion to organs and tissues. High blood pressure may increase strain on the heart and blood vessels over time.

Temperature

Temperature helps identify fever, infection, inflammation, or abnormal metabolic activity. Elevated temperature may point to illness or inflammation, while low temperature may occur with cold exposure, shock, or severe illness.

Oxygen Saturation

Oxygen saturation shows how much hemoglobin is carrying oxygen. A low reading may suggest respiratory compromise, poor gas exchange, airway problems, or equipment issues such as poor sensor placement.

How Vital Signs Help in Real Clinical Assessment

Vital signs are more than numbers to memorize. They often provide the first clue that a patient is improving, declining, compensating, or becoming unstable. A rising heart rate, falling blood pressure, low oxygen saturation, or fast respiratory rate can point to bleeding, infection, dehydration, respiratory distress, pain, or shock.

Healthcare learners should combine vital signs with symptoms, appearance, age, baseline condition, and overall patient presentation. Looking at patterns matters more than reacting to a single isolated number.

How to Think About Vital Signs on Exams

On exams like the TEAS, nursing tests, or healthcare fundamentals quizzes, vital signs are often used to test recognition rather than memorization.

  • A rising heart rate with low blood pressure may suggest shock or poor perfusion.
  • A fast respiratory rate may indicate early distress.
  • Low oxygen saturation may signal impaired oxygenation or poor sensor reading.
  • Fever combined with tachycardia may suggest infection or increased metabolic demand.
  • Low blood pressure with dizziness or weakness may suggest poor perfusion.
What to notice first:

Look for the vital sign that does not match the patient situation. That is usually the clue to the correct answer.

Common Abnormal Vital Signs

Recognizing abnormal vital signs early can help learners understand when a patient may need closer monitoring, additional assessment, or escalation.

  • Tachycardia: Heart rate above 100 bpm in adults. Common causes include pain, fever, dehydration, anxiety, bleeding, and shock.
  • Bradycardia: Heart rate below 60 bpm in adults. This may be normal in trained athletes, but it can also occur with medications or heart conduction problems.
  • Tachypnea: Respiratory rate above normal. This can signal respiratory distress, fever, anxiety, pain, or metabolic acidosis.
  • Bradypnea: Respiratory rate below normal. This may occur with sedation, narcotic overdose, or neurologic impairment.
  • Fever: A temperature above 100.4°F generally suggests infection or inflammation.
  • Hypotension: Low blood pressure may lead to dizziness, weakness, poor perfusion, or shock.
  • Hypertension: Elevated blood pressure can increase cardiovascular risk and may require follow-up or treatment.
  • Low SpO2: Oxygen saturation below expected levels may suggest hypoxemia, respiratory disease, airway problems, poor sensor placement, or poor perfusion.
Important:

One abnormal vital sign does not always tell the whole story. Trends over time, symptoms, baseline values, and the full patient picture matter.

Vital Signs and TEAS / Nursing Study

Vital signs appear often in nursing, TEAS, medical assisting, EMT, and healthcare fundamentals because they connect directly to patient assessment.

For TEAS prep, vital signs reinforce science concepts like circulation, oxygenation, body temperature regulation, respiratory function, and homeostasis.

For nursing students, vital signs help build clinical judgment. The goal is not just to know the normal range. The goal is to recognize when a value does not fit the patient situation.

What to notice first:

In exam questions, look for the vital sign trend or abnormal value that changes the priority.

Study Tips for Remembering Vital Signs

  • Memorize normal adult ranges first before moving into pediatric ranges.
  • Focus on patterns instead of isolated numbers.
  • Practice matching abnormal values to likely causes.
  • Review pediatric values by age group instead of trying to learn all child ranges at once.
  • Use practice questions and quizzes to reinforce recall.
  • Learn what abnormal values mean, not just whether they are high or low.
Study strategy:

Do not only memorize “normal.” Also practice recognizing what high, low, fast, slow, rising, or falling values could mean.

Frequently Asked Questions

What are normal adult vital signs?

Normal adult vital signs typically include a heart rate of 60–100 bpm, respiratory rate of 12–20 breaths per minute, blood pressure around 90/60 to 120/80 mmHg, temperature around 97–99°F, and oxygen saturation of 95–100%.

What is a normal pediatric heart rate?

A normal pediatric heart rate depends on age. Infants usually have faster heart rates than older children and adolescents.

Why is respiratory rate so important?

Respiratory rate can change early when a patient is getting worse. It may rise before other vital signs show a problem, which makes it especially important in patient assessment.

What blood pressure is considered low?

In adults, blood pressure below about 90/60 mmHg is often considered low, but the significance depends on symptoms, baseline values, and the overall patient condition.

What does low oxygen saturation mean?

Low oxygen saturation may suggest that the body is not getting enough oxygen. It can be caused by respiratory issues, poor gas exchange, airway problems, poor perfusion, or an incorrect pulse oximeter reading.
