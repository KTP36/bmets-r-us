What Is the CBET Exam?

The CBET certification is designed for biomedical equipment technicians and healthcare technology professionals. It validates knowledge related to medical equipment, electronics, troubleshooting, safety, maintenance, and clinical technology support.

MedSkillBuilder is not a certification provider. The goal here is to help learners build practical understanding before test day through active recall, scenario-based questions, and repeated practice.

Key Topics to Practice

Electrical Safety

Leakage current, grounding, inspection, equipment risk, and safe service decisions.

Patient Monitoring

ECG, SpO₂, NIBP, alarms, sensors, cables, signal quality, and patient monitor function.

Therapeutic Equipment

Infusion pumps, defibrillators, ventilators, suction, electrosurgery, and clinical use.

Electronics

Voltage, current, resistance, Ohm’s law, capacitors, rectifiers, fuses, switches, and circuits.

Troubleshooting

Symptoms, likely causes, first checks, connection issues, user setup, and system behavior.

PM & Quality Control

Inspection, preventive maintenance, documentation, performance verification, and testing.

CBET Troubleshooting Framework

Many CBET-style questions are really troubleshooting questions. A good BMET does not randomly replace parts. A good BMET confirms the complaint, checks simple causes first, isolates the failure, verifies repair, and documents clearly.

Confirm the problem. Ask what happened, when it happened, who saw it, and whether the issue can be repeated.
Check setup and accessories. Many problems come from cables, electrodes, sensors, batteries, tubing, settings, or user setup.
Inspect for obvious damage. Look for cracked housings, damaged cords, bent pins, fluid intrusion, missing parts, or alarm messages.
Isolate the system. Determine whether the problem is the device, accessory, power source, network, patient connection, or environment.
Test safely. Use approved test equipment, follow service procedures, and protect patients and staff.
Verify and document. Confirm performance after repair and document what was found, corrected, tested, and returned to service.
Safety rule: if equipment may present risk to a patient or user, remove it from service until it is evaluated.

CBET Electronics Deep Dive: DC, AC, Ohm's Law, and Power

Electronics questions are some of the most missed CBET-style questions because they require understanding relationships, not just memorizing definitions. This section strengthens the foundation behind voltage, current, resistance, power, AC/DC, series circuits, and parallel circuits.

Ohm's Law for CBET Candidates

Formula Use It When Example
V = I × R You know current and resistance and need voltage. 2 A × 10 Ω = 20 V
I = V ÷ R You know voltage and resistance and need current. 12 V ÷ 6 Ω = 2 A
R = V ÷ I You know voltage and current and need resistance. 24 V ÷ 2 A = 12 Ω
CBET tip: If resistance increases and voltage stays the same, current decreases.

Power Formulas

Formula Meaning
P = V × I Power equals voltage times current.
P = I² × R Useful when current and resistance are known.
P = V² ÷ R Useful when voltage and resistance are known.

AC vs DC Current

Type Meaning CBET Relevance
AC Alternating current changes direction. Wall power, transformers, line power safety.
DC Direct current flows one direction. Batteries, internal circuits, many electronics boards.

Series vs Parallel Circuits

Circuit Type What Happens Exam Clue
Series Current has one path. Total resistance increases as components are added. One open component can stop the whole path.
Parallel Current has multiple paths. Total resistance decreases as branches are added. One branch can fail while others still operate.

CBET Components: Fuses, Diodes, Capacitors, Transformers, and Rectifiers

Biomedical equipment technicians often troubleshoot power and signal problems by understanding what components are supposed to do. You do not need to design the circuit, but you should know the function of common parts.

Component Primary Function Common Failure Clue
Fuse Opens a circuit during excessive current. Device has no power; fuse open after fault.
Diode Allows current flow in one direction. Power supply or rectification problem.
Rectifier Converts AC to DC. DC output missing or unstable.
Capacitor Stores electrical energy and filters ripple. Bulging, leaking, poor filtering, failure to hold charge.
Transformer Changes AC voltage and may provide isolation. No secondary output, overheating, hum, open winding.
Relay Electrically controlled switch. Clicking, stuck contacts, load not switching.
Safety note: capacitors can hold charge even after power is removed. Always follow service procedures before working inside equipment.

CBET Electronics Practice Questions

9. A 12 V source is connected to a 6 Ω resistor. What is the current?

A. 0.5 A   B. 2 A   C. 6 A   D. 72 A

Answer: B. 2 A
Use I = V ÷ R. 12 ÷ 6 = 2 amps.
10. In a series circuit, what happens to total resistance when another resistor is added?

A. It decreases   B. It increases   C. It becomes zero   D. It becomes AC only

Answer: B. It increases
Series resistances add together, increasing total resistance.
11. In a parallel circuit, what happens to total resistance when another branch is added?

A. It usually decreases   B. It always increases   C. It becomes infinite   D. It changes to voltage

Answer: A. It usually decreases
Adding parallel paths allows more current flow, reducing equivalent resistance.
12. Which component is commonly used to smooth DC ripple in a power supply?

A. Capacitor   B. Speaker   C. Keyboard   D. Bed rail

Answer: A. Capacitor
Capacitors can store charge and help smooth voltage ripple.
13. Which component allows current to flow mostly in one direction?

A. Diode   B. Resistor only   C. Fuse only   D. Transformer only

Answer: A. Diode
A diode is a one-way semiconductor device.
14. What is the likely purpose of a fuse in medical equipment?

A. Store patient data   B. Protect against excessive current   C. Increase oxygen   D. Measure blood pressure

Answer: B. Protect against excessive current
A fuse opens the circuit when current exceeds its rating.
15. What does a transformer work with?

A. AC   B. DC only   C. Oxygen flow only   D. NIBP cuffs only

Answer: A. AC
Transformers require changing magnetic fields, so they are used with AC.
16. A device has an open power cord ground. What is the safest action?

A. Return it to use   B. Remove it from service until corrected   C. Ignore it if battery works   D. Increase alarm volume

Answer: B. Remove it from service until corrected
Ground integrity is a safety issue and must be corrected before use.

Imaging Systems for CBET Prep: X-Ray, CT, MRI, Ultrasound, PACS & DICOM

Imaging equipment appears throughout biomedical and clinical engineering work. CBET candidates should understand basic imaging concepts, safety risks, common failures, and the difference between image acquisition, image display, and image storage.

Radiation safety principle: minimize time, maximize distance, and use shielding when appropriate.

CT Basics

Computed tomography uses rotating X-ray equipment and computer reconstruction to create cross-sectional images.

CT ComponentPurpose

MRI Safety Basics

MRI does not use ionizing radiation, but it has major magnetic field safety risks. Metal objects, implants, monitoring equipment, and oxygen cylinders require careful screening.

MRI RiskWhy It Matters
Projectile effectFerromagnetic objects can be pulled into the magnet.
ImplantsSome implants are unsafe or conditional in MRI.
RF heatingConductive loops or leads may heat tissue.
QuenchRapid loss of superconductivity releases cryogens.
MRI rule: never bring unknown equipment into the MRI environment without confirming MRI safety status.

Ultrasound Basics

Ultrasound uses high-frequency sound waves rather than ionizing radiation. Common BMET issues include probe damage, cable breaks, image artifacts, trackball problems, and system boot failures.

IssueLikely Cause
Dropout artifactDamaged probe elements or cable issue.
No image from probeProbe, connector, port, or software selection issue.
Trackball not respondingDirt, wear, or input device failure.
Poor image qualityProbe damage, incorrect preset, user setup, or system issue.

PACS and DICOM

Modern imaging depends on networks. Images must be acquired, stored, routed, viewed, and archived correctly.

TermMeaningCBET Relevance
DICOMImaging communication standard.Image transfer and modality communication.
PACSPicture Archiving and Communication System.Image storage and retrieval.
Modality WorklistPatient/order data sent to imaging device.Prevents demographic entry errors.
HL7Healthcare data messaging standard.Orders/results workflow context.

Imaging Troubleshooting Flow

Clarify the failure. Is it acquisition, image quality, network transfer, storage, or display?
Check user setup. Confirm patient selection, exam type, preset, and workflow steps.
Inspect accessories. Probes, detectors, cables, coils, and connections are common failure points.
Check network status. Confirm DICOM destination, IP settings, worklist, and PACS availability when images do not transfer.
Escalate safely. Imaging systems often require vendor support, radiation safety, MRI safety, or clinical engineering escalation.

Patient Monitor Troubleshooting Flow

Confirm complaint. Verify the reported issue.
Inspect accessories. Leads, cuffs, probes and sensors fail more often than monitors.
Inspect cables. Look for bent pins, stretched wires and contamination.
Use simulator. Verify monitor operation with appropriate test equipment.
Document findings. Record failure, repair and final testing.

Therapeutic Equipment: Infusion Pumps, Defibrillators & Electrosurgery

Therapeutic devices are high-risk systems because they directly deliver energy, medication, or treatment to the patient. CBET candidates should understand the clinical purpose, common failure modes, first troubleshooting steps, and safety checks for each device.

Device Primary Function Common Failure First Check
Infusion Pump Delivers fluids and medications at a controlled rate. Occlusion alarms, door or latch problems, battery failure, flow inaccuracy. Administration set, tubing, cassette, door, battery, and programming setup.
Defibrillator Delivers electrical energy to treat life-threatening cardiac rhythms. Charging failure, low battery, paddle or cable issue, failed energy output. Battery, pads or paddles, cables, charge cycle, and self-test results.
Electrosurgical Unit Uses high-frequency electrical energy for cutting and coagulation. No output, accessory fault, return electrode alarm, damaged cable. Active electrode, return electrode, cables, footswitch, mode, and settings.
Sequential Compression Device Supports venous blood flow and helps reduce clot risk. Air leak, hose disconnect, sleeve problem, pump failure. Sleeves, tubing, connectors, pressure output, and alarms.
High-risk equipment rule: verify performance with the correct analyzer or test load before returning the device to clinical service.

Infusion Pump Troubleshooting

Confirm the complaint. Identify the alarm, medication set, flow rate, and when the problem occurs.
Inspect disposables. Check tubing, cassette, clamps, air-in-line sensors, and the door or latch.
Check battery and power. Confirm charging, runtime, and operation on AC and battery.
Verify flow accuracy. Use the approved infusion device analyzer and service procedure.
Document and trend. Repeated failures may indicate a fleet-wide accessory or maintenance issue.

Defibrillator Functional Checks

  • Inspect pads, paddles, cables, and connectors.
  • Verify charge time and delivered energy with a defibrillator analyzer.
  • Check synchronized cardioversion and pacing functions when applicable.
  • Verify battery condition, alarms, printer, and self-test status.
  • Confirm ECG acquisition and lead integrity.

Electrosurgical Safety

Safety Concern Why It Matters
Return electrode contact Poor contact may increase burn risk.
Damaged active cable Insulation failure can create unintended energy delivery.
Wrong mode or power setting May cause poor clinical performance or tissue injury.
Ignition sources Electrosurgery can contribute to operating-room fire risk.

Electrical Safety, Preventive Maintenance & Performance Verification

Electrical safety is one of the most important responsibilities of a Biomedical Equipment Technician. Understanding leakage current, grounding, preventive maintenance, and documentation are essential for both the CBET exam and real-world hospital practice.

Topic Purpose What to Verify
Leakage Current Protect patients and staff from unintended electrical current. Verify values are within manufacturer and safety standards.
Ground Resistance Confirms proper equipment grounding. Inspect power cord, ground pin, and resistance measurement.
Line Isolation Monitor (LIM) Monitors isolated power systems in operating rooms. Alarm operation and hazard current indication.
Preventive Maintenance Detect failures before clinical use. Inspection, cleaning, calibration, safety tests, documentation.
Performance Verification Confirms repairs were successful. Functional testing, analyzer verification, alarm testing.
High-Yield CBET Electrical Safety Tips
  • Never return equipment to service without completing electrical safety testing when required.
  • Document every repair, calibration, and preventive maintenance activity.
  • Always use the manufacturer's service manual and approved procedures.
  • Verify alarms after every major repair.
  • When troubleshooting, inspect the simplest causes before replacing expensive components.

Preventive Maintenance Checklist

  • Visual inspection for damage
  • Verify power cord integrity
  • Inspect accessories and cables
  • Electrical safety testing
  • Functional verification
  • Alarm testing
  • Battery evaluation (if applicable)
  • Cleaning and preventive maintenance documentation
