What Is Ohm's Law?

Ohm's Law explains how circuits actually behave. It connects voltage, current, and resistance so troubleshooting stops feeling random and starts making sense.

This is one of the highest-value beginner electronics concepts for CBET prep, biomedical equipment troubleshooting, and understanding power supply behavior.

ICurrent flow
RResistance opposition

Quick Answer

Ohm's Law explains the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance.
V = I × R
Voltage = Current × Resistance
  • V means voltage, or electrical pressure.
  • I means current, or electrical flow.
  • R means resistance, or opposition to flow.
What to notice first: If voltage goes up while resistance stays the same, current goes up. If resistance goes up while voltage stays the same, current goes down.

The Formula

The main Ohm's Law formula is:

V = I × R

But you can rearrange the formula depending on what you need to find.

To Find Formula Meaning
Voltage V = I × R Voltage equals current multiplied by resistance.
Current I = V ÷ R Current equals voltage divided by resistance.
Resistance R = V ÷ I Resistance equals voltage divided by current.

What This Actually Means

Ohm's Law shows how these three values affect each other:

  • If voltage increases and resistance stays the same, current increases.
  • If resistance increases and voltage stays the same, current decreases.
  • If resistance decreases and voltage stays the same, current increases.

This is the foundation of how circuits respond when something changes.

If you need a deeper breakdown of each term, review: Voltage vs Current vs Resistance.

To see how this applies in real testing, review: How to Use a Multimeter.

Simple pattern: Voltage pushes, resistance limits, current changes.

Simple Calculation Examples

Find Current

If voltage is 100V and resistance is 10Ω:

I = V ÷ R

I = 100 ÷ 10 = 10 amps

Find Voltage

If current is 2A and resistance is 6Ω:

V = I × R

V = 2 × 6 = 12 volts

Find Resistance

If voltage is 24V and current is 3A:

R = V ÷ I

R = 24 ÷ 3 = 8 ohms

Tip: The math matters, but the behavior matters more. Always ask what changed first.

Why This Matters in Medical Equipment

Ohm's Law helps technicians understand why a device is underperforming, why current is too high, why components overheat, why readings are unstable, and why a circuit may fail under load.

Real-world example:

A device has correct voltage, but current is lower than expected. That points toward resistance being too high somewhere in the circuit. Instead of guessing, Ohm's Law gives you direction.

  • Power supplies depend on predictable voltage and current delivery.
  • Fuses may blow if current becomes too high.
  • Connections, components, and circuit paths can create unwanted resistance.
  • Stable device operation depends on stable electrical behavior.

This applies to monitors, power supplies, imaging systems, infusion pumps, charging circuits, and more.

Common Mistakes

  • Thinking voltage alone determines current.
  • Forgetting that resistance controls flow.
  • Memorizing the formula without understanding behavior.
  • Assuming higher resistance creates higher current.
  • Skipping units when doing calculations.
Common mistake: Higher resistance does not increase current. It makes current flow harder.

How to Rearrange Ohm's Law Without Guessing

Many learners can remember V = I × R but freeze when the question asks for current or resistance. The safest method is to start with the original relationship and isolate the value you need.

Find Voltage

V = I × R

Multiply current by resistance.

Example: 3A × 8Ω = 24V

Find Current

I = V ÷ R

Divide voltage by resistance.

Example: 24V ÷ 8Ω = 3A

Find Resistance

R = V ÷ I

Divide voltage by current.

Example: 24V ÷ 3A = 8Ω

CBET strategy: Write the known values with units before choosing a formula. This reduces mistakes and makes unit conversion problems easier to spot.

Ohm's Law Units You Must Recognize

Ohm's Law questions often become harder because the values are written in milliamps, millivolts, kilohms, or megohms. Convert the values before calculating unless the units already match.

Quantity Base Unit Common Prefix Conversion
Voltage Volt (V) Millivolt (mV) 1,000 mV = 1 V
Current Ampere (A) Milliampere (mA) 1,000 mA = 1 A
Current Ampere (A) Microampere (µA) 1,000,000 µA = 1 A
Resistance Ohm (Ω) Kilohm (kΩ) 1 kΩ = 1,000 Ω
Resistance Ohm (Ω) Megohm (MΩ) 1 MΩ = 1,000,000 Ω
Example:

A 12V circuit has a resistance of 4kΩ. Convert 4kΩ to 4,000Ω, then calculate: I = 12 ÷ 4,000 = 0.003A, or 3mA.

What Changes When Voltage, Current, or Resistance Changes?

Understanding the direction of change is often more important than doing the arithmetic. Many CBET questions ask what will increase or decrease after one variable changes.

Condition What Stays Constant Result
Voltage increases Resistance Current increases
Voltage decreases Resistance Current decreases
Resistance increases Voltage Current decreases
Resistance decreases Voltage Current increases
Current increases Resistance Voltage must increase
Fast memory rule: Voltage and current move in the same direction when resistance is constant. Resistance and current move in opposite directions when voltage is constant.

Ohm's Law and Electrical Power

Ohm's Law is often tested together with electrical power. Power describes how quickly electrical energy is used or converted.

P = V × I
Power = Voltage × Current

By combining the power formula with Ohm's Law, two additional equations can be derived:

Voltage and Current Known

P = V × I

12V × 2A = 24W

Current and Resistance Known

P = I² × R

2A² × 6Ω = 24W

Voltage and Resistance Known

P = V² ÷ R

12V² ÷ 6Ω = 24W

Why this matters: Excessive current increases heat production. That is why low-resistance faults can damage wiring, connectors, circuit boards, and power components.

Series Circuits and Ohm's Law

In a series circuit, there is only one path for current. The same current flows through every component.

  • Total resistance equals the sum of all resistances.
  • Current is the same at every point in the circuit.
  • Source voltage is divided across the components.
  • If one component opens, current stops everywhere.
RT = R1 + R2 + R3
Example:

A 24V source powers three series resistors: 2Ω, 4Ω, and 6Ω. Total resistance is 12Ω. Current is 24 ÷ 12 = 2A.

Parallel Circuits and Ohm's Law

In a parallel circuit, multiple current paths share the same source voltage.

  • Voltage is the same across every branch.
  • Branch currents may be different.
  • Total current equals the sum of branch currents.
  • Total resistance is always less than the smallest branch resistance.
1 ÷ RT = 1 ÷ R1 + 1 ÷ R2 + 1 ÷ R3
Example:

Two equal 12Ω resistors are connected in parallel. Their combined resistance is 6Ω. If connected to 12V, total current is 2A.

Open Circuits, Short Circuits, and High-Resistance Faults

Open Circuit

Resistance becomes extremely high.

Current drops to zero.

Possible causes include broken wires, blown fuses, and disconnected connectors.

Short Circuit

Resistance becomes extremely low.

Current rises rapidly.

Possible results include blown fuses, tripped protection, heat, or component failure.

High-Resistance Fault

Resistance rises above its intended value.

Current decreases.

Possible causes include corrosion, loose terminals, damaged contacts, or deteriorated solder joints.

Using a Multimeter With Ohm's Law

A multimeter gives you real measurements that can be compared with the values predicted by Ohm's Law.

Measure Voltage in Parallel

Place the meter leads across the component or source. Voltage can normally be measured while the circuit is energized, but follow all manufacturer procedures and safety precautions.

Measure Current in Series

The meter becomes part of the current path. Current measurement is more invasive and may require opening the circuit. Selecting the wrong jack or range can damage the meter or circuit.

Measure Resistance With Power Off

Resistance should generally be measured only after power is removed and stored energy is safely discharged. Other circuit paths may affect an in-circuit resistance reading.

Safety reminder: Never place a meter configured for current measurement directly across a voltage source.

Medical Equipment Troubleshooting Examples

Infusion Pump Motor:

The supply voltage is correct, but the motor receives less current than expected. Increased resistance in a connector, cable, motor winding, or driver circuit may be limiting current.

Patient Monitor Power Rail:

A 5V rail falls when the display activates. The circuit may be drawing excessive current, or a power supply component may be unable to maintain voltage under load.

Battery Charging Circuit:

Correct source voltage does not guarantee correct charging current. Battery condition, cable resistance, current-limiting components, and charger control circuits all affect current flow.

Heating Element:

A resistive heater draws current and converts electrical power into heat. A change in element resistance changes both current and heating performance.

A Step-by-Step Troubleshooting Method

  1. Verify the complaint. Confirm the failure and identify when it occurs.
  2. Inspect first. Look for damaged cables, loose connectors, corrosion, heat damage, and contamination.
  3. Check the source. Verify the expected supply voltage.
  4. Check behavior under load. A supply may look normal with no load and collapse when current is demanded.
  5. Compare expected and measured values. Use the schematic, service literature, or known-good data.
  6. Use Ohm's Law. Determine whether resistance or current is consistent with the measured voltage.
  7. Isolate the circuit. Narrow the problem to a board, connector, cable, component, or load.
  8. Verify the repair. Retest the device under normal operating conditions.
Best practice: Use Ohm's Law to support your diagnosis, not to replace the manufacturer troubleshooting procedure.

Common CBET Exam Traps

  • Using milliamps as though they were amps.
  • Using kilohms as though they were ohms.
  • Multiplying when the question requires division.
  • Ignoring what variable stays constant.
  • Assuming current is used up as it moves through a series circuit.
  • Assuming voltage is identical across all series components.
  • Forgetting that total parallel resistance is less than the smallest branch resistance.
  • Trying to measure resistance in an energized circuit.
  • Confusing electrical power in watts with electrical energy in watt-hours.

Worked Ohm's Law Problems

Problem 1: A circuit has 48V and 16Ω. Find current.
Answer: 3A
I = V ÷ R = 48 ÷ 16 = 3A.
Problem 2: A device draws 500mA from a 12V supply. Find resistance.
Answer: 24Ω
Convert 500mA to 0.5A. R = V ÷ I = 12 ÷ 0.5 = 24Ω.
Problem 3: A 2kΩ resistor carries 5mA. Find voltage.
Answer: 10V
Convert 2kΩ to 2,000Ω and 5mA to 0.005A. V = I × R = 0.005 × 2,000 = 10V.
Problem 4: A 120V circuit draws 2A. Find power.
Answer: 240W
P = V × I = 120 × 2 = 240W.
Problem 5: Resistance doubles while voltage stays constant. What happens to current?
Answer: Current is cut in half.
Current and resistance are inversely related when voltage is constant.
Problem 6: Voltage doubles while resistance stays constant. What happens to current?
Answer: Current doubles.
Current changes directly with voltage when resistance remains unchanged.

Power Supply Diagnostic Walkthrough

When a medical device will not power correctly, avoid replacing boards immediately. Verify the input voltage, measure the output under load, compare expected current with measured current, and use Ohm's Law to determine whether the fault is likely in the source, wiring, or load.

Case Study: Patient Monitor

A 12V rail drops to 8V only after the display powers on.

Likely cause: excessive load or a weak power supply.
A no-load measurement can look normal even when the supply cannot deliver enough current.

Fuse Troubleshooting Guide

  • Never replace a fuse with a higher-rated fuse.
  • Determine why the fuse opened before restoring power.
  • Look for shorts, damaged insulation, failed components, or excessive current draw.
  • Verify current after the repair.
CBET Tip: A fuse is evidence of a problem—not the problem itself.

Think Like a Biomedical Technician

  2. Check power.
  3. Measure voltage.
  4. Measure current when appropriate.
  5. Use Ohm's Law to explain the measurements.
  6. Repair and verify.

Resistance vs. Impedance in AC Circuits

Ohm's Law still helps in alternating-current circuits, but resistance alone may not describe everything opposing current. Capacitors and inductors create frequency-dependent opposition called reactance. The combined opposition is called impedance and is measured in ohms.

Term Where It Appears What It Describes
Resistance (R) DC and AC circuits Opposition that converts electrical energy primarily into heat.
Capacitive Reactance (XC) AC circuits with capacitors Opposition that decreases as frequency increases.
Inductive Reactance (XL) AC circuits with coils or transformers Opposition that increases as frequency increases.
Impedance (Z) AC circuits The total opposition created by resistance and reactance.
I = V ÷ Z
AC Current = Voltage ÷ Impedance
CBET takeaway: For a purely resistive load, impedance and resistance may be treated similarly. In circuits containing capacitors, inductors, transformers, or frequency-sensitive loads, impedance gives the more complete picture.

Why Diodes, LEDs, and Transistors Are Different

A standard resistor is approximately linear: doubling the applied voltage roughly doubles the current when resistance remains constant. Semiconductor devices do not always behave this way. Their effective resistance changes with voltage, current, temperature, and operating region.

Diode

Primarily allows current in one direction.

Very little forward current flows until the junction reaches its forward-voltage region.

LED

A light-emitting diode with a characteristic forward-voltage range.

A current-limiting resistor is normally required to prevent excessive current.

Transistor

Can act as an electronic switch or amplifier.

A small control signal can influence a much larger circuit current.

LED resistor example:

A 5V supply powers an LED with a 2V forward drop. The desired current is 10mA. The resistor must drop the remaining 3V. Convert 10mA to 0.01A: R = 3V ÷ 0.01A = 300Ω. A nearby standard value such as 330Ω may be selected after checking the design requirements.

Important: Ohm's Law is still used around semiconductor devices, but you must account for the device's voltage drop and operating behavior.

Common Digital Multimeter Mistakes

Correct calculations are useless if the measurement was taken incorrectly. These mistakes can produce false conclusions, damage a meter, damage equipment, or create a safety hazard.

Mistake Why It Matters Better Practice
Leaving the red lead in the current jack The next voltage measurement may create a near-short through the meter. Return the lead to the voltage/ohms jack immediately after measuring current.
Measuring resistance on a powered circuit The meter supplies its own test signal and external voltage can create an invalid reading or damage. Remove power, discharge stored energy, and confirm the circuit is safe.
Placing an ammeter directly across a source An ammeter has very low internal resistance and may cause excessive current. Open the circuit and connect the meter in series.
Ignoring meter range or category rating The meter may overload or be unsuitable for the available energy level. Select an appropriate range and use a meter rated for the measurement environment.
Trusting an in-circuit resistance reading without context Parallel paths can make the measured resistance lower than the component's actual value. Compare with the schematic and isolate one component lead when necessary and permitted.
Using continuity as a complete circuit test A circuit may beep for continuity yet fail under normal current demand. Check resistance, voltage drop, and performance under load.
Technician habit: Before touching the probes to a circuit, confirm the function, lead position, expected value, range, and safe connection method.

Biomedical Equipment Case Studies

Use each scenario to connect measured voltage, current, and resistance with a practical troubleshooting decision.

Case 1: Infusion Pump Will Not Charge

The charger output is 15V with no battery connected. When the battery is connected, the output falls to 6V and the charger becomes warm.

Most likely direction: investigate excessive load current, a failing battery, or a weak charger.
Correct no-load voltage does not prove the supply can maintain voltage while delivering current. Isolate the battery and charger according to the service procedure.
Case 2: Patient Monitor Has an Intermittent Display

The display works when the cable is moved. Supply voltage is correct at the power board but varies at the display connector.

Most likely direction: inspect the cable, connector contacts, and solder joints for intermittent resistance.
A damaged connection can create changing resistance and voltage drop as the cable moves.
Case 3: Ventilator Blower Starts Slowly

The motor supply measures 24V with the motor disconnected but falls to 17V during startup. Startup current is above specification.

Most likely direction: inspect for mechanical binding, motor failure, or a weak supply.
A stalled or heavily loaded motor can draw excessive current. The increased current can cause supply voltage to sag.
Case 4: Defibrillator Will Not Complete Charging

The low-voltage supply is normal, but the charging current is below the manufacturer's expected range.

Most likely direction: follow the service manual to evaluate the charging path, switching circuit, connections, and energy-storage components.
Low charging current can result from excessive resistance, an open path, a control problem, or a component that is not being driven correctly.
Case 5: Surgical Light Is Dim

Source voltage is correct. A voltage-drop measurement across a connector shows 2.5V while the lamp is operating.

Most likely direction: repair or replace the high-resistance connector according to the approved procedure.
A healthy closed connector should not consume a large portion of the supply voltage. Voltage across the connection indicates unwanted resistance under load.
Case 6: Ultrasound System Reboots During Imaging

Standby measurements are normal. During high processing demand, a power rail drops and ripple increases.

Most likely direction: evaluate the power supply under load and inspect filtering components and high-current connections.
Dynamic failures may appear only when current demand increases. Static voltage checks can miss them.
Case 7: ECG Lead Reports Frequent Disconnects

Visual inspection is normal, but conductor resistance changes when the lead wire is flexed.

Most likely direction: replace the damaged lead assembly.
A partially broken conductor creates intermittent resistance that can interrupt the low-level ECG signal.
Case 8: Battery Operated Device Has Short Runtime

Battery voltage appears acceptable at rest but drops sharply when the device begins operating.

Most likely direction: perform the approved battery capacity or load test.
Increased internal resistance can allow normal open-circuit voltage while causing excessive voltage drop under load.
Case 9: Portable X-Ray Unit Blows an Input Fuse

A replacement fuse opens immediately when the system is powered.

Most likely direction: stop replacing fuses and isolate the overcurrent fault.
Repeated fuse operation indicates excessive current. Installing a larger fuse or bypassing protection is unsafe.
Case 10: Warming Device Heats Slowly

Supply voltage is within specification, but the heating element's measured resistance is much higher than the service value.

Most likely direction: evaluate the element and its connections for deterioration or partial failure.
At constant voltage, increased resistance reduces current and lowers electrical power delivered to the heater.

Read the Meter: Troubleshooting Exercises

Exercise 1: 12V Source, 4Ω Load, 3A Measured

Does the measurement agree with Ohm's Law?

Yes. Expected current is 12 ÷ 4 = 3A.
The measured current matches the predicted value.
Exercise 2: 12V Source, 4Ω Intended Load, 1A Measured

What effective resistance does the measurement indicate?

12Ω.
R = V ÷ I = 12 ÷ 1 = 12Ω. The circuit has more resistance than expected, so inspect the load and series connections.
Exercise 3: 24V Supply Falls to 18V at 2A

What is the approximate internal or series resistance causing the 6V loss?

3Ω.
R = V ÷ I = 6V ÷ 2A = 3Ω of effective unwanted series resistance.
Exercise 4: 5V Across a 1kΩ Resistor

What current should flow?

5mA.
I = 5 ÷ 1,000 = 0.005A, which equals 5mA.
Exercise 5: 10mA Through a 470Ω Resistor

What voltage should appear across the resistor?

4.7V.
Convert 10mA to 0.01A. V = 0.01 × 470 = 4.7V.
Exercise 6: Connector Drops 1.2V While Carrying 0.4A

What is the connector's effective resistance?

3Ω.
R = 1.2 ÷ 0.4 = 3Ω. That would be excessive for many power connectors and could create heat.

0.05A, or 50mA.
Total resistance is 320Ω. I = 16 ÷ 320 = 0.05A.
Problem 10: Two 100Ω resistors are connected in parallel across 10V. Find total current.
0.2A, or 200mA.
Two equal 100Ω resistors in parallel equal 50Ω. I = 10 ÷ 50 = 0.2A.
Problem 11: A resistor dissipates 2W while carrying 0.2A. Find resistance.
50Ω.
Use P = I²R. R = P ÷ I² = 2 ÷ 0.04 = 50Ω.
Problem 12: A 12V circuit should draw 300mA but draws 600mA. Compare the effective resistances.
Expected resistance = 40Ω; measured effective resistance = 20Ω.
Expected: 12 ÷ 0.3 = 40Ω. Actual: 12 ÷ 0.6 = 20Ω. The lower effective resistance explains the doubled current.

Advanced CBET-Style Questions: 31–40

31. A 24V supply powers a 48Ω load. What current should flow?

A. 0.25A   B. 0.5A   C. 2A   D. 72A

Answer: B. 0.5A
I = V ÷ R = 24 ÷ 48 = 0.5A.
32. Which condition can make a battery show normal voltage at rest but fail under load?

A. Low internal resistance   B. Increased internal resistance   C. Infinite capacity   D. Perfect connections

Answer: B. Increased internal resistance
Internal resistance creates a larger voltage drop when current is demanded.
33. A connector drops 2V while carrying 0.5A. What is its effective resistance?

A. 0.25Ω   B. 1Ω   C. 4Ω   D. 10Ω

Answer: C. 4Ω
R = V ÷ I = 2 ÷ 0.5 = 4Ω.
34. Why can continuity mode miss a failing power connection?

A. Continuity always measures voltage   B. A connection may pass a tiny test current but fail under load   C. Continuity creates AC power   D. The meter removes resistance

Answer: B. A connection may pass a tiny test current but fail under load
Voltage-drop testing under normal current demand may reveal resistance that continuity mode does not.
35. In an AC circuit, what term describes total opposition to current?

A. Capacitance   B. Impedance   C. Conductance only   D. Charge

Answer: B. Impedance
Impedance combines resistance and reactance.
36. Why is a resistor commonly placed in series with an LED?

A. To increase source voltage   B. To limit current   C. To reverse polarity   D. To create an open circuit

Answer: B. To limit current
Once forward biased, an LED can draw damaging current without a limiting element.
37. A supply measures correctly until the load is connected. What measurement is especially useful next?

A. Voltage under load   B. Room temperature only   C. Fuse color   D. Device weight

Answer: A. Voltage under load
A source may have normal open-circuit voltage but collapse when current is demanded.
38. A 5V circuit draws 20mA. What is the equivalent resistance?

A. 25Ω   B. 100Ω   C. 250Ω   D. 2,500Ω

Answer: C. 250Ω
20mA = 0.02A. R = 5 ÷ 0.02 = 250Ω.
39. Which action is unsafe when a fuse repeatedly opens?

A. Inspecting for shorts   B. Measuring current according to procedure   C. Installing a larger fuse   D. Reviewing the schematic

Answer: C. Installing a larger fuse
A larger fuse defeats the intended protection and may allow damaging current.
40. A 12V heater's resistance increases from 6Ω to 12Ω. What happens to power?

A. It doubles   B. It is cut in half   C. It remains constant   D. It becomes zero

Answer: B. It is cut in half
P = V² ÷ R. At 6Ω, power is 24W. At 12Ω, power is 12W.

When Ohm's Law Is Not Enough

Ohm's Law remains foundational, but advanced troubleshooting may also require waveform analysis, frequency response, component specifications, control signals, temperature behavior, software logs, and manufacturer diagnostic procedures.

  • Diodes and LEDs: account for forward voltage and nonlinear current behavior.
  • Transistors: identify whether the device is switching, amplifying, saturated, or cut off.
  • Capacitors: evaluate charge, discharge, capacitance, leakage, equivalent series resistance, and frequency.
  • Inductors and transformers: consider inductance, reactance, winding resistance, coupling, and frequency.
  • Digital circuits: logic thresholds and timing may matter more than a simple equivalent resistance.
  • Regulated power supplies: feedback, protection, ripple, switching operation, and load response affect behavior.
The bigger lesson: Ohm's Law helps you interpret electrical evidence. The schematic and service documentation tell you how the complete system is intended to work.
