What Is Ohm’s Law?

Most people misunderstand this.

Ohm’s Law explains how circuits actually behave. It connects voltage, current, and resistance—and once you understand it, troubleshooting stops feeling random and starts making sense.

🔧 The Formula

V = I × R

  • V (Voltage) → electrical pressure
  • I (Current) → flow of electrons
  • R (Resistance) → opposition to flow

🧠 What This Actually Means

Ohm’s Law shows how these three values affect each other:

  • If voltage increases → current increases
  • If resistance increases → current decreases
  • If resistance decreases → current increases

This is the foundation of how circuits respond when something changes.

If you need a deeper breakdown of each term, review: voltage vs current vs resistance.

To see how this applies in real testing, review: how to use a multimeter.

🔍 Real-World Example

A device is not working correctly:

  • Voltage is correct
  • But current is low

This tells you resistance is likely too high somewhere in the circuit.

Instead of guessing, Ohm’s Law gives you direction.

This is also why component issues matter. A failing part like a capacitor can change resistance and disrupt current flow: learn how to test a capacitor.

📊 Simple Calculation Example

If:

  • Voltage = 100V
  • Resistance = 10Ω

Then:

Current = 10 Amps

⚠️ Common Mistakes

  • Thinking voltage alone determines current
  • Forgetting resistance controls flow
  • Memorizing the formula without understanding behavior
Common mistake: Higher resistance does NOT increase current—it makes current flow harder.

🔧 Why This Matters in Medical Equipment

Ohm’s Law helps technicians understand:

  • Why a device is underperforming
  • Why components overheat
  • Why readings are unstable

This applies to monitors, power supplies, imaging systems, and more.
