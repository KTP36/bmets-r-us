What Does a Capacitor Do?

A capacitor is an electronic component that stores and releases electrical energy. In simple terms, it acts like a temporary storage device inside a circuit.

Capacitors are commonly used to smooth voltage, filter signals, reduce electrical noise, and provide short bursts of energy when needed.

Simple Explanation

Think of a capacitor like a small electrical storage tank.

  • When voltage is applied, the capacitor charges.
  • When the circuit needs energy, the capacitor can discharge.
  • This charge and discharge behavior helps stabilize circuits.

How a Capacitor Works

A capacitor contains two conductive plates separated by an insulating material called a dielectric.

When voltage is applied across the plates, electrical energy is stored in an electric field. When the circuit allows it, that stored energy is released back into the circuit.

Why Capacitors Matter in Power Supplies

One of the most important uses of capacitors is in power supplies.

After AC voltage is converted into DC by a rectifier, the DC signal may still have ripple. A capacitor helps smooth that ripple so the circuit receives a more stable DC voltage.

That is why capacitors and rectifiers are often learned together.

Why Capacitors Matter in Medical Equipment

Capacitors are used in many types of medical equipment, including monitors, power supplies, imaging systems, and defibrillators.

In a defibrillator, capacitors store electrical energy and release it rapidly when a shock is delivered. In other devices, capacitors may help stabilize power, filter noise, or support timing circuits.

Real-World Example

If a device has unstable power, intermittent operation, or a power supply issue, a capacitor may be part of the problem.

A failed capacitor may be shorted, open, leaking, swollen, or unable to hold charge properly.

How a Capacitor May Fail

  • Shorted capacitor: may show very low resistance and can disrupt the circuit.
  • Open capacitor: may not charge or store energy properly.
  • Weak capacitor: may still work partially but fail under load.
  • Physical damage: swelling, leaking, or bulging can indicate failure.

Basic Multimeter Clue

When checking a capacitor with resistance mode, the reading may start low and then rise as the capacitor charges.

If the reading stays near zero, the capacitor may be shorted. If it stays open, it may not be charging properly.

Common Beginner Mistakes

  • Thinking capacitors create energy instead of storing it
  • Forgetting that capacitors can hold a charge after power is removed
  • Confusing a capacitor with a resistor
  • Testing components without confirming the circuit is safe
  • Ignoring physical signs like bulging or leaking

CBET-Style Questions to Know

  • Which component stores electrical energy?
  • Which component helps smooth ripple in a DC power supply?
  • What happens when a capacitor charges?
  • Why are capacitors important in defibrillators?

The answer in each case points back to the capacitor’s main job: storing and releasing electrical energy.

Key Takeaways

  • A capacitor stores energy in an electric field.
  • Capacitors charge and discharge in a circuit.
  • They help smooth voltage and filter noise.
  • They are important in medical equipment power supplies and defibrillators.
  • A capacitor can fail open, shorted, weak, or physically damaged.
