Electronics Master Review for the CBET Exam

This section builds the electronics foundation that every CBET candidate should understand. Focus on concepts rather than memorizing formulas.

Core Formulas

FormulaMeaning
V = I × ROhm's Law
P = V × IElectrical Power
I = V ÷ RCurrent
R = V ÷ IResistance

Series vs. Parallel

  • Series: One current path. Total resistance increases.
  • Parallel: Multiple current paths. Total resistance decreases.

Component Review

ComponentFunction

Electrical Safety & Patient Protection

Electrical safety is one of the most important responsibilities of a Biomedical Equipment Technician. A CBET candidate should understand not only how equipment functions, but how to identify conditions that may place patients or staff at risk.

High-Yield Safety Concepts

Concept Description Exam Tip
Grounding Provides a low-resistance path for fault current. Always inspect the power cord and ground pin first.
Leakage Current Small unintended electrical current that may flow from equipment. Verify using an electrical safety analyzer.
Protective Earth Helps protect users from electrical shock. Never ignore a broken ground connection.
Isolation Separates hazardous voltages from patients. Understand isolated power concepts.

Preventive Maintenance Checklist

  1. Visual inspection
  2. Power cord inspection
  3. Ground integrity verification
  4. Leakage current testing
  5. Functional performance verification
  6. Calibration (if required)
  7. Final documentation

Electrical Safety Troubleshooting

  • Never return equipment to service after failing electrical safety testing.
  • Replace damaged power cords immediately.
  • Inspect connectors for bent pins or corrosion.
  • Look for signs of fluid intrusion.
  • Verify repairs using approved biomedical test equipment.

CBET Safety Practice Questions

11. What should be performed after most repairs before returning equipment to clinical use?
Answer: Functional verification and electrical safety testing.

12. A missing ground pin represents what type of concern?
Answer: A patient and user electrical safety hazard.

13. What instrument is commonly used to measure leakage current?
Answer: An electrical safety analyzer.

14. During preventive maintenance, what should always be documented?
Answer: Findings, repairs, testing performed, and return-to-service status.

15. Which should be investigated before replacing an expensive circuit board?
Answer: Power source, accessories, cables, connectors, settings, and external failures.

CBET Exam Tip:
The safest answer is usually the correct answer. Many CBET questions reward systematic troubleshooting and patient safety over the quickest repair.

PACS and DICOM

DICOM is the standard for storing and transmitting medical images. PACS archives, routes, and retrieves those images throughout the hospital.

  • Verify network connectivity before replacing hardware.
  • Check DICOM destination settings when images fail to transmit.
  • Confirm modality worklist communication when patient information is missing.

Radiation Safety Principles

  1. Minimize exposure time.
  2. Maximize distance from the radiation source.
  3. Use appropriate shielding.
  4. Follow departmental radiation safety policies.
MRI Safety Pearl: Never assume equipment is MRI-safe. Verify the MRI safety classification before bringing any device into the magnet room.

Imaging Troubleshooting Workflow

  1. Confirm the reported failure.
  2. Determine whether the problem is acquisition, display, storage, or networking.
  3. Inspect detectors, probes, coils, and cables.
  4. Verify DICOM communication and PACS connectivity.
  5. Complete functional testing and document all findings.

Preventive Maintenance Priorities

  • Visual inspection and cleanliness.
  • Accessory inspection (hoses, cables, sensors, probes).
  • Electrical safety testing when applicable.
  • Performance verification with approved analyzers.
  • Battery evaluation and replacement when required.
  • Complete documentation in the CMMS.

Equipment Troubleshooting Cases

Case 1: Ventilator fails pre-use test.
First actions: Inspect breathing circuit, filters, valves, and leak points before suspecting internal failure.

Case 2: Infusion pump repeatedly alarms for occlusion.
First actions: Inspect tubing, cassette, downstream obstruction, and door latch before replacing hardware.

Case 3: Defibrillator battery runtime is poor.
First actions: Verify battery health, charger operation, and perform an energy delivery test.

Case 4: ESU reports return electrode alarm.
First actions: Verify pad placement, cable integrity, and contact quality.

Cybersecurity Best Practices

  • Keep operating systems and approved software updated.
  • Remove unsupported devices from clinical use or isolate them.
  • Use strong passwords and approved authentication methods.
  • Report suspected malware or ransomware immediately.
  • Follow hospital change-control procedures before installing software.
Cybersecurity Pearl: A perfectly functioning medical device can still become unsafe if its software or network security is compromised.

  • Document troubleshooting steps.
  • Record parts replaced.
  • Include verification testing.
  • Document return-to-service status.

Real-World Biomedical Troubleshooting Case Studies

These scenarios mirror the style of thinking expected from experienced BMETs. Focus on a logical process instead of jumping directly to component replacement.

Case 1 – Patient Monitor Will Not Power On

  • Complaint: ICU monitor is completely dead.
  • Check first: Wall outlet, power cord, AC inlet, fuse, battery status.
  • Lesson: Always eliminate simple external causes before opening the device.

Case 2 – Ventilator Fails Leak Test

  • Inspect breathing circuit, expiratory valve, filters, humidifier, and external tubing.
  • Only investigate internal components after external leaks are ruled out.

Case 3 – Infusion Pump Occlusion Alarm

  • Inspect tubing, cassette, downstream clamp, and door latch.
  • Verify performance with an infusion device analyzer.

Case 4 – ECG Artifact

  • Replace electrodes.
  • Inspect lead wires.
  • Check patient movement and electrical interference.
  • Use a simulator if needed.

Case 5 – Ultrasound Image Dropout

  • Inspect probe face, cable strain relief, connector, and perform probe testing.

Universal Troubleshooting Decision Tree

  1. Verify the complaint.
  2. Inspect accessories.
  3. Inspect power and connections.
  4. Attempt to reproduce the issue safely.
  5. Use approved analyzers or simulators.
  6. Repair only after isolating the failure.
  7. Verify operation.
  8. Complete documentation.

Scenario-Based CBET Questions

36. A monitor intermittently loses ECG. What is the most likely first failure? Electrodes or lead wires.

37. A ventilator suddenly fails leak testing after tubing replacement. What should be checked first? External breathing circuit assembly.

38. A networked infusion pump cannot communicate with the server. What should be verified before replacing hardware? Network connectivity and IP configuration.

39. A defibrillator passes self-test but battery runtime is poor. What should be evaluated? Battery health and charger performance.

40. Which troubleshooting philosophy best reflects CBET practice? Start simple, isolate the failure, verify the repair, and document the results.

Final Exam Tip: CBET questions frequently ask for the best first action. The safest, most systematic answer is usually correct.
