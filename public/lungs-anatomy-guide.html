Lungs Anatomy Guide

Learn the major structures of the lungs and airways with this simple anatomy guide. Review the trachea, carina, bronchi, lobes of the lungs, alveoli, gas exchange, oxygenation, and breathing basics for class, anatomy quizzes, TEAS review, nursing study, and healthcare exam prep.

What to notice first:

The lungs are not just structures to memorize. Their main job is to move oxygen into the body and remove carbon dioxide. That connection between anatomy and function is what matters most on exams and in real patient care.

What Do the Lungs Do?

The lungs are the main organs of the respiratory system. Their job is to bring oxygen into the body and remove carbon dioxide. Air travels through the upper airway, down the trachea, through the bronchi, and into the lungs where gas exchange takes place.

Learning lungs anatomy helps students understand breathing, airway structure, oxygen delivery, and respiratory function. It is especially useful for anatomy students, nursing students, TEAS review, and general healthcare exam preparation.

The lungs work closely with the heart and blood vessels. Oxygen enters the bloodstream through the lungs, and carbon dioxide leaves the bloodstream through the lungs. This is why respiratory problems can affect the entire body quickly.

If you want to practice identifying these structures after reviewing this guide, you can also try the Lungs Anatomy Quiz for a more active way to study.

What to notice first:

If oxygen is not getting in or carbon dioxide is not getting out, think respiratory problem.

How Air Moves Through the Respiratory Tract

A simple way to understand lungs anatomy is to follow the path of air. Air enters through the nose or mouth, moves through the throat, passes down the trachea, reaches the carina, and then splits into the right and left main bronchi. From there, air continues into smaller branching airways inside the lungs until it reaches tiny air sacs called alveoli, where oxygen and carbon dioxide are exchanged.

This step-by-step pathway helps connect anatomy to function. When students understand where air travels, it becomes easier to understand respiratory assessment, breathing problems, and why airway anatomy matters in clinical care.

  • Nose or mouth: Air enters the body.
  • Trachea: Air moves down the main airway.
  • Carina: The airway splits into the right and left main bronchi.
  • Bronchi: Air moves into each lung.
  • Smaller airways: Air travels deeper into the lungs.
  • Alveoli: Gas exchange takes place.
What to notice first:

If airflow is blocked anywhere along this pathway, oxygen delivery can be affected. This is why airway problems can become serious quickly.

Main Lung Structures

Trachea

The trachea is the main airway tube that carries air from the throat toward the lungs.

Carina

The carina is the point where the trachea splits into the right and left main bronchi.

Right Main Bronchus

The right main bronchus carries air from the trachea into the right lung.

Left Main Bronchus

The left main bronchus carries air from the trachea into the left lung.

Right Lung

The right lung is larger and typically has three lobes: upper, middle, and lower.

Left Lung

The left lung is slightly smaller to make room for the heart and usually has two lobes.

Alveoli

Alveoli are tiny air sacs where oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide leaves the blood.

Pleura

The pleura is a thin lining around the lungs that helps reduce friction during breathing.

Quick study tip: Remember that the right lung is usually larger and has three lobes, while the left lung is smaller and has two lobes because the heart takes up space on the left side.

Gas Exchange in the Alveoli

Gas exchange happens in the alveoli. Alveoli are tiny air sacs surrounded by small blood vessels called capillaries. Oxygen moves from the alveoli into the blood, and carbon dioxide moves from the blood into the alveoli so it can be breathed out.

This is one of the most important lung concepts for healthcare learners. The airway brings air into the lungs, but the alveoli are where oxygen actually crosses into the bloodstream.

  • Oxygen: moves from the alveoli into the blood.
  • Carbon dioxide: moves from the blood into the alveoli.
  • Breathing out: removes carbon dioxide from the body.
What to notice first:

Alveoli are the key gas exchange site. If gas exchange is impaired, oxygen levels may drop and carbon dioxide may build up.

Right Lung vs Left Lung

The right and left lungs are similar, but they are not exactly the same. The right lung is typically larger and has three lobes. The left lung is smaller because the heart sits slightly toward the left side of the chest.

  • Right lung: upper lobe, middle lobe, lower lobe.
  • Left lung: upper lobe and lower lobe.
  • Left side: has space for the heart.
Memory tip:

Right lung = 3 lobes. Left lung = 2 lobes.

Why Lung Anatomy Matters

Understanding lungs anatomy supports learning about breathing, oxygenation, airway function, and common respiratory conditions. It also helps students connect anatomy with clinical topics like wheezing, shortness of breath, pneumonia, collapsed lung, and airway obstruction.

For healthcare learners, lungs anatomy is more than a memorization topic. It supports patient assessment, oxygen delivery concepts, respiratory monitoring, and interpretation of symptoms involving the chest and airway. This is one reason lung anatomy shows up so often in anatomy classes, nursing study, and entrance exam review.

Lung anatomy also connects closely to vital signs and ABGs. A patient with breathing difficulty may have a high respiratory rate, low oxygen saturation, abnormal breath sounds, or abnormal arterial blood gas values.

What to notice first:

Always connect lung anatomy to oxygenation. Anatomy tells you the structure, but oxygenation tells you how well the system is working.

Clinical Connections: What Lung Problems Can Look Like

Lung problems often show up through changes in breathing, oxygen levels, breath sounds, or patient appearance. These are the types of clues that commonly appear in nursing, TEAS, and healthcare fundamentals questions.

  • Wheezing: may suggest narrowed airways.
  • Crackles: may suggest fluid in the lungs.
  • Low SpO2: may suggest impaired oxygenation.
  • Fast breathing: may be compensation for low oxygen, fever, anxiety, pain, or metabolic problems.
  • Shortness of breath: may indicate respiratory distress or poor gas exchange.
What to notice first:

Respiratory problems often show up as changes in respiratory rate and oxygen saturation.

Lungs Anatomy and Exam Study

On anatomy quizzes, TEAS questions, and nursing exams, lung anatomy questions often test recognition. You may be asked to identify the trachea, bronchi, carina, right lung, left lung, or alveoli. You may also be asked to connect a structure to its function.

  • Trachea: main airway.
  • Carina: split point of the trachea.
  • Bronchi: carry air into the lungs.
  • Alveoli: gas exchange.
  • Right lung: three lobes.
  • Left lung: two lobes.
What to notice first:

Most exam questions test whether you can recognize the structure and connect it to the correct function.

Lungs Anatomy Practice Questions

  1. What is the main function of the lungs?
    • A. Pump blood
    • B. Exchange oxygen and carbon dioxide
    • C. Digest food
    • D. Filter waste
    Answer: B. Exchange oxygen and carbon dioxide — The lungs bring in oxygen and remove carbon dioxide.
  2. Which structure carries air from the throat toward the bronchi?
    • A. Carina
    • B. Trachea
    • C. Esophagus
    • D. Aorta
    Answer: B. Trachea — The trachea is the main airway leading to the lungs.
  3. What is the carina?
    • A. The lining of the lungs
    • B. The top of the throat
    • C. The point where the trachea splits
    • D. A chamber of the heart
    Answer: C. The point where the trachea splits — The carina divides the airway into the main bronchi.
  4. Which bronchus carries air into the right lung?
    • A. Left main bronchus
    • B. Right main bronchus
    • C. Trachea
    • D. Carina
    Answer: B. Right main bronchus — It directs air into the right lung.
  5. Which lung is slightly smaller?
    • A. Right lung
    • B. Left lung
    • C. Both are equal size
    • D. Neither lung
    Answer: B. Left lung — The left lung is smaller to make room for the heart.
  6. Which lung usually has three lobes?
    • A. Left lung
    • B. Right lung
    • C. Both lungs
    • D. Neither lung
    Answer: B. Right lung — The right lung is typically divided into three lobes.
  7. Where does gas exchange occur?
    • A. Trachea
    • B. Carina
    • C. Alveoli
    • D. Esophagus
    Answer: C. Alveoli — Alveoli are the tiny air sacs where oxygen and carbon dioxide exchange occurs.
  8. Which structure splits into the right and left main bronchi?
    • A. Trachea at the carina
    • B. Left lung
    • C. Alveoli
    • D. Diaphragm
    Answer: A. Trachea at the carina — The carina is the split point into the main bronchi.
