Lesson 7.1

Equipment Safety and Readiness

Safe equipment use begins with inspection. Before using a device, verify that it is clean, intact, appropriate for the patient, powered correctly, and ready to perform as expected.

InspectCheck cords, plugs, sensors, cuffs, tubing, and surfaces
PrepareUse the correct accessories and settings
VerifyConfirm power, battery, self-test, and readiness
ProtectKeep patients and staff away from hazards
Remove From Service: Do not use equipment with exposed wires, damaged plugs, cracked housings, fluid intrusion, missing parts, failed self-tests, or unreliable readings.

Lesson 7.2

Stethoscopes and Blood Pressure Equipment

Stethoscopes, manual sphygmomanometers, and automated blood pressure monitors are common tools for patient assessment. Accuracy depends on correct cuff size, placement, patient position, and equipment condition.

EquipmentKey Safety or Accuracy Point
StethoscopeClean earpieces and diaphragm; inspect tubing for cracks.
Manual BP cuffUse the correct cuff size and inspect the bladder and tubing.
Automated BP monitorUse approved cuffs and verify the device completes its self-check.
Clinical Pearl: A cuff that is too small may produce a falsely high blood pressure reading; a cuff that is too large may produce a falsely low reading.

Lesson 7.3

Thermometers and Pulse Oximeters

Thermometers and pulse oximeters provide quick measurements, but technique and patient factors can affect accuracy.

Thermometer Considerations

  • Select the approved route and probe cover.
  • Use the correct site and positioning.
  • Clean the device according to manufacturer instructions.
  • Consider recent eating, drinking, smoking, or activity when relevant.

Pulse Oximeter Considerations

  • Use a warm, still finger with good circulation.
  • Remove barriers such as heavy nail polish when policy requires.
  • Confirm the sensor is positioned correctly.
  • Compare the reading with the patient's appearance and symptoms.

Clinical Scenario

A pulse oximeter displays 82%, but the patient is talking comfortably and the pulse waveform is weak and irregular.

Best response: Reposition the sensor, warm the hand, check another site if allowed, and promptly report persistent abnormal readings.

Lesson 7.4

ECG Machines and Electrodes

ECG quality depends on patient preparation, correct lead placement, intact cables, fresh electrodes, and minimizing electrical and movement artifact.

ECG Preparation Checklist

  • Verify the patient and order.
  • Explain the procedure and provide privacy.
  • Prepare clean, dry skin.
  • Place electrodes at the correct anatomical locations.
  • Inspect lead wires and connectors.
  • Ask the patient to remain still and relaxed.
  • Review the tracing for artifact before completion.
Artifact Reminder: Movement, loose electrodes, poor skin contact, electrical interference, and damaged cables can distort the tracing.
Equipment Concern: If a lead wire is damaged or the machine repeatedly fails self-testing, remove it from service and follow the reporting process.

Lesson 7.5

Centrifuges and Laboratory Equipment

Centrifuges separate specimen components by spinning at high speed. Improper loading can damage specimens, equipment, or nearby staff.

Safe Centrifuge Use

  1. Inspect the rotor, tube holders, lid, and power cord.
  2. Use compatible tubes and caps.
  3. Balance tubes with equal volume opposite each other.
  4. Close and lock the lid before starting.
  5. Do not open the lid until the rotor stops completely.
  6. Clean spills according to exposure-control policy.
Stop Immediately: Excessive vibration, unusual noise, burning odor, smoke, or a failed lid lock requires immediate shutdown and removal from service.

Lesson 7.6

Autoclaves and Sterilization Systems

Autoclaves use pressurized steam to sterilize approved instruments. Proper loading, cycle selection, monitoring, drying, and storage are essential.

StepPurpose
Cleaning before sterilizationRemoves soil that can interfere with sterilization.
Correct packagingAllows steam penetration and protects instruments afterward.
Chemical indicatorShows the package was exposed to process conditions.
Biological monitoringProvides evidence that the sterilization process can kill resistant organisms.
Dry storageProtects package integrity after processing.
Do Not Use: Wet, torn, opened, punctured, or otherwise compromised sterile packages should not be used as sterile supplies.

Lesson 7.7

Cleaning, Disinfection, and Storage

Equipment cleaning must follow the device manufacturer's instructions and the facility's infection-prevention policies. The correct product, contact time, compatibility, and drying process matter.

CleanRemove visible soil
DisinfectUse an approved product and full contact time
DryPrevent moisture damage and contamination
StoreProtect from dust, damage, and unauthorized use
Compatibility Matters: Some chemicals damage plastics, sensors, screens, adhesives, or electronic components. Follow manufacturer instructions.

Lesson 7.8

Troubleshooting, Documentation, and Escalation

Medical assistants may perform basic user-level checks, but should not attempt repairs beyond training and policy. Unsafe or unreliable devices should be removed from patient use.

Basic User-Level Checks

  • Confirm the power source or battery status.
  • Check that accessories are correctly connected.
  • Verify the correct setting or mode.
  • Repeat a self-test when permitted.
  • Replace approved disposable accessories if needed.
  • Compare the result with another approved method when directed.

Document and Report

  • Device name and identifying number
  • Date and time
  • Problem observed
  • Patient impact, if any
  • Actions taken
  • Person or department notified
Clinical Pearl: Label unsafe equipment clearly, remove it from service, prevent accidental reuse, and notify the responsible department.

1. What should be done with equipment that has an exposed wire?
Damaged electrical equipment should be removed from service and reported.
2. A blood pressure cuff that is too small may cause which error?
A cuff that is too small can produce a falsely high blood pressure reading.
3. Which action may improve a poor pulse-oximeter signal?
Warming the hand and repositioning the sensor may improve signal quality.
4. What commonly causes artifact on an ECG tracing?
Movement and poor electrode contact are common causes of ECG artifact.
5. Why must centrifuge tubes be balanced?
Balanced loading reduces vibration and helps protect the centrifuge and specimens.
6. When may a centrifuge lid be opened?
Wait until the rotor has stopped completely before opening the lid.
7. What should happen if a centrifuge begins vibrating violently?
Excessive vibration indicates a potential loading or equipment problem and requires stopping the device.
8. Why are instruments cleaned before autoclaving?
Cleaning removes soil that may protect microorganisms or interfere with sterilization.
9. Which package should not be considered sterile?
Wet, torn, opened, or punctured packages may have lost sterility.
10. What is the purpose of a disinfectant contact time?
The surface must remain wet for the full approved contact time.
11. Why must manufacturer cleaning instructions be followed?
Cleaning products and methods must be compatible with the device.
12. Which is an appropriate user-level troubleshooting step?
Checking power, batteries, settings, and accessory connections is appropriate user-level troubleshooting.
13. What should happen after a device repeatedly fails its self-test?
Repeated self-test failures indicate the device may be unsafe or unreliable.
14. Which information belongs in an equipment incident report?
Equipment documentation should be specific and traceable.
15. What is the best way to prevent accidental reuse of unsafe equipment?
Clearly label unsafe equipment and remove it from service to prevent accidental reuse.
