How to Use a Multimeter

A multimeter is one of the most important tools in electronics and biomedical equipment. It helps you measure voltage, resistance, and continuity so you can understand what is happening in a circuit.

This beginner guide shows what to measure, where to place the probes, what the reading means, and the mistakes to avoid.

Quick Answer

A multimeter is used to measure electrical values such as voltage, resistance, and continuity.

Think of a multimeter as a health check tool for circuits. It helps answer simple troubleshooting questions:

  • Is power present?
  • Is the circuit path complete?
  • Is a component open, shorted, or out of range?
  • Is voltage AC or DC?
Safety first: Always know what you are measuring before touching the probes to a circuit. Resistance and continuity checks should be done with power off and capacitors discharged.

What Is a Multimeter?

A multimeter is a device used to test electrical values. It is one of the first tools beginners should learn because it connects directly to voltage, current, resistance, continuity, power supplies, batteries, wires, fuses, and component checks.

Voltage

Measures electrical pressure or potential. This helps confirm whether power is present.

Resistance

Measures opposition to current flow. This can help check components, wires, and circuit paths.

Continuity

Checks whether a path is complete. A beep usually means the path is connected.

To understand the terms behind meter readings, review Voltage vs Current vs Resistance.

Parts of a Multimeter

Part What It Does Beginner Note
Display Shows the measurement reading. Always check the unit shown, such as V, Ω, or continuity.
Dial Selects what you are measuring. Wrong dial setting is one of the most common mistakes.
Black lead Reference lead. Normally goes in the COM port.
Red lead Measurement lead. Usually goes in the V/Ω port for voltage, resistance, and continuity.
What to notice first: The black lead usually stays in COM. The red lead location depends on what you are measuring.

How to Measure Voltage

Voltage testing helps answer one of the most important troubleshooting questions: is power present?

  1. Turn the dial to voltage, shown as V.
  2. Select AC voltage or DC voltage if your meter requires it.
  3. Place the probes across the two points being measured.
  4. Read the value on the display.
Example: A AA battery should read around 1.5V DC when it is healthy.
Probe placement: Voltage is measured across a component or source, also called parallel measurement.

AC Voltage vs DC Voltage

Setting Used For Example
AC Voltage Alternating current power. Wall outlet power.
DC Voltage Direct current power. Batteries and internal electronics.

Review AC vs DC Current if you are not sure which setting to choose.

How to Measure Resistance

Resistance testing helps you understand whether a component or path is resisting current as expected.

  1. Turn the dial to Ω.
  2. Turn power off to the circuit.
  3. Properly discharge capacitors if present.
  4. Place probes across the component.
  5. Read the resistance value.
Never measure resistance on a live circuit. The meter sends a small test signal during resistance measurement. External voltage can damage the meter, damage the circuit, or create unsafe conditions.

Review Ohm's Law to understand how resistance affects current.

Continuity Check

Continuity mode checks whether a low-resistance path exists between two points. Many meters beep when continuity is present.

  1. Turn the dial to the continuity symbol or beep setting.
  2. Touch the probes together first to confirm the meter beeps.
  3. Test the wire, fuse, switch, connector, or path.
  4. Listen for the beep or watch the display.
Beep usually means: the path is complete.
No beep may mean: the path is open, broken, switched off, or higher resistance than the continuity threshold.

Common Mistakes

  • Testing resistance on a live circuit.
  • Using the wrong dial setting.
  • Using the wrong probe port.
  • Forgetting to choose AC or DC voltage.
  • Measuring voltage as if it were continuity.
  • Assuming a continuity beep proves every part is good.
  • Ignoring capacitor stored charge after power is removed.
High-value safety reminder: A circuit can still be dangerous after power is removed if capacitors remain charged.

Real-World Troubleshooting Thinking

If a device is not working, a multimeter helps you stop guessing and start isolating the problem.

Check voltage

Is power present at the outlet, power supply, battery, or board input?

Check continuity

Are cables, fuses, switches, connectors, and paths intact?

Check resistance

Does a component or path look open, shorted, or out of expected range?

Field mindset: Start with the simplest question first. Is power present? Then ask if the path is complete and whether the load makes sense.

Multimeter Use in Biomedical Equipment

Biomedical equipment technicians use multimeters to support safe, practical troubleshooting. The goal is not to randomly probe a device. The goal is to understand the circuit, choose the correct setting, and test safely.

  • Checking battery voltage
  • Verifying power supply output
  • Checking fuses
  • Testing cable continuity
  • Looking for opens or shorts
  • Confirming whether voltage is AC or DC

CBET-Style Quick Questions

1. Which port does the black lead usually go into?

A. COM   B. V/Ω only   C. Current port always   D. Display

Answer: A. COM
The black lead normally goes into the COM port.
2. What setting is used to measure resistance?

A. Ω   B. V only   C. Hz only   D. Temperature only

Answer: A. Ω
Resistance is measured in ohms, shown with the Ω symbol.
3. Should resistance be measured on a live circuit?

A. Yes, always   B. No, power should be off   C. Only if the screen is bright   D. Only on batteries

Answer: B. No, power should be off
Resistance checks should be done with power off and stored energy safely discharged.
4. What does continuity mode help check?

A. Whether a path is complete   B. Patient age   C. Oxygen saturation   D. The color of a wire only

Answer: A. Whether a path is complete
Continuity checks for a complete low-resistance path.
5. What should you check before measuring wall outlet power?

A. AC voltage setting and proper safety precautions   B. Resistance mode   C. Continuity beep only   D. Capacitance only

Answer: A. AC voltage setting and proper safety precautions
Wall outlets use AC voltage and require appropriate safety precautions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What does a multimeter measure?

A multimeter can measure voltage, resistance, continuity, and often current depending on the meter and setup.

Where does the black lead go?

The black lead normally goes in the COM port.

Can I measure resistance on a live circuit?

No. Resistance should be measured with power off and stored energy safely discharged.

What does a continuity beep mean?

A continuity beep usually means a complete low-resistance path exists between the two test points.
